Session 03

Context Windows and Context Management

How Claude Code's working memory works — and how to manage it for rigorous research

Kai Zhu — Bocconi University — Spring 2026

Welcome to Session 3. Today's concept is the single most useful one for day-to-day Claude Code use: the context window — Claude's finite working memory. Every dropped citation, every forgotten instruction, every contradiction you will ever see traces back to it.

Three claims for today. The window is finite and leaky. Effective context is smaller than nominal. And you have six concrete techniques to manage it, including subagents that isolate verbose work entirely.

By the end, you will have felt the window fill up and you will leave with a context-hygiene checklist for your dissertation.

The Problem: Your AI's Working Memory is Finite and Leaky

A research scenario you will recognize:

  • Hour 1: You ask Claude to help screen 30 abstracts for a systematic review. It is precise, catches exclusion criteria, and justifies each decision.
  • Hour 2: You ask it to synthesize the survivors into a thematic map. The synthesis is fine, but Claude starts hedging and repeating itself.
  • Hour 3: You ask for a summary table of methods. Claude invents two citations and drops an inclusion rule you stated at the start.
⚠️

This is not laziness. This is context exhaustion.

The same model, the same prompts, the same you — but the working memory filled up, and then got silently edited to fit.

Take this seriously as a methodological problem, not a UX annoyance. If a human RA invented citations in hour three of a literature screen, you would throw out that hour's work. When Claude does it, apply the same standard — but most people miss it, because the prose is fluent and the confidence is high, and there is no visible moment when Claude "switched modes."

The reason to master today's material is not efficiency. It is rigor. If you shipped hour-three output to a published paper and a reviewer later found invented citations, the reputational cost is not recoverable. Today's job is to make the invisible transition visible, and to give you the tools to prevent it.

What the Context Window Actually Is

The context window is a single shared buffer of tokens.

At every turn, Claude sees this buffer and nothing else:

  • System prompt (Anthropic's instructions to the model)
  • Your CLAUDE.md file(s)
  • Tool definitions (Bash, Read, Write, etc.)
  • Every message you have sent
  • Every response Claude has produced
  • Every file Claude has read and every tool output
💡

Size

Claude Opus 4.6: 200,000 tokens by default (≈ 150,000 English words, ≈ 300 pages). A 1M-token beta is available for select tiers. Every piece of text above counts against this budget.

Three things to internalize. One: "context window" is not a metaphor. It is a literal token buffer Claude reads on every turn. No hidden memory, no persistent store. If you want Claude to know something, it has to be inside this buffer, right now, this turn.

Two: everything shares the same budget. A 50,000-token PDF is not free — it crowds out room for your messages and Claude's responses. Load another file and you are at half capacity before any analysis begins.

Three: 200,000 tokens sounds like a lot, but it is not. One long article with supplements is thirty pages; every tool call adds more; and as we will see in three slides, effective performance degrades well before the ceiling. The 1M-token beta does not change that. The canonical docs are on platform.claude.com if you want to verify the numbers.

Why the Window is Fixed: The Transformer Bottleneck

The context window ceiling is not a software setting. It is an architectural constraint rooted in how transformers work.

  • Self-attention is quadratic. Every token "checks" every other token. For n tokens, that is n2 comparisons per layer. At 200k tokens: 40 billion attention scores per layer.
  • Memory scales with length. The model caches key-value pairs for every past token (the KV cache). For a large model, that is ≈2–3 MB per token — at 200k tokens, hundreds of gigabytes of GPU RAM.
  • Position encodings have a training range. The model learns positions up to a fixed ceiling (e.g., 200k). Beyond that, it encounters positions it has never seen, and performance degrades. Extensions exist (YaRN, position interpolation) but always trade off quality.
💡

The dinner-party analogy

Imagine each token is a dinner guest who must have a brief one-on-one exchange with every other guest before dessert. With 10 guests: 45 exchanges. With 200,000 guests: 20 billion. Double the guests, quadruple the exchanges. That is why the bill grows so fast.

This slide answers the natural question: why not just make the window bigger? The answer traces to Vaswani et al. (2017), the transformer paper. Self-attention computes a score between every pair of tokens — that is what makes it powerful, but the cost is quadratic: n² comparisons. At 1,000 tokens that is 1 million scores; at 200,000 it is 40 billion. Double the context, quadruple the compute.

On top of that, the KV cache stores key-value vectors from every previous token — roughly 2–3 MB per token for a large model. At 200k tokens, that is hundreds of gigabytes for a single conversation, more than any single GPU holds.

And positional encoding is trained, not mathematical law. Extrapolating beyond the training range degrades performance. The bottom line: the window is finite because physics and mathematics say so, not because Anthropic chose to limit it. When you manage context, you are working within a real constraint.

Mental Model: The Working Desk

Imagine Claude is a researcher working at a physical desk:

  • The desk surface = the current turn. Everything Claude can look at right now.
  • The desk drawer = your session. It fills up as you pile on books, notes, and print-outs. Once it is full, something has to go.
  • The pinned note above the desk = your CLAUDE.md. Claude re-reads it every single turn, and it is never thrown out.
  • A junior researcher next door = a subagent. They have their own desk and their own drawer. You hand them a task; they hand back a one-paragraph summary.
💡

Why this matters

Every decision about what to put on the desk and when to clear it is a methodological decision. Context management is a research skill, not a UI skill.

Carry the desk metaphor with you for the rest of the course. Desk surface is this turn — what Claude is looking at right now. Drawer is the session. Pinned note is CLAUDE.md, re-read every turn, never thrown out. Junior researcher next door is a subagent, with their own desk that never interferes with yours.

The broader point, and it matters: context management is not a UI skill. It is a methodological decision about what information is relevant to the current reasoning step. Clearing the desk mid-project is the same kind of judgment call you apply when you structure a research design. Students who treat context management as a methods problem become much better users of AI than students who treat it as tool use.

Nominal Context vs. Effective Context

A model with a 200k-token window does not reliably use 200k tokens.

  • Nominal context length: the vendor-advertised ceiling (200k for Claude Opus 4.6, 1M for the beta).
  • Effective context length: the longest input at which the model still performs the task reliably. Typically far below the nominal.
💡

Two well-known empirical results

  • Lost in the middle (Liu et al., TACL 2024): accuracy on long-document QA follows a U-shape — highest when the key passage is near the beginning or end, lowest in the middle, even for long-context models.
  • Context rot (Hong, Troynikov, & Huber, Chroma 2025): across 18 frontier LLMs (Claude 4, GPT-4.1, Gemini 2.5), task performance degrades non-uniformly as input length grows — with difficulty held constant. We will discuss this paper today.

The most counterintuitive point in the lecture, and the one you will most need to defend to colleagues. When vendors advertise a 1M-token context, they mean the model will not reject the input — not that it will use those tokens as reliably as it uses 10k.

Liu et al. 2024 in TACL is the canonical citation. Clean experiment: put a known answer somewhere in a long context and vary the position. Accuracy follows a sharp U-curve, lowest in the middle. That figure is everywhere in long-context research now.

Chroma's Context Rot this afternoon extends this to frontier models you actually use — Claude 4, GPT-4.1, Gemini 2.5. So the results apply to you directly. The implication: you cannot just dump everything in. You have to curate. The rest of today is about how.

How Claude Code Loads Context

When you type claude and press enter in a project folder, Claude Code silently loads all of this into the window:

  1. The system prompt (Anthropic's instructions).
  2. Any CLAUDE.md files found by walking upward from your current directory to the filesystem root.
  3. Any CLAUDE.md in your home directory (~/.claude/CLAUDE.md).
  4. Tool definitions for Bash, Read, Write, Grep, and so on.

Then every turn adds your message, Claude's response, and any tool outputs.

You can see what is in the window at any moment:

/context

Context-loading is invisible but not free. When you open Claude Code in a folder, it walks upward to the filesystem root, loads every CLAUDE.md it finds along the way, plus the global one in your home directory. So a 5,000-token global CLAUDE.md is a tax on every session you start, anywhere. It adds up faster than you expect.

The /context command is your window into what is loaded right now: a breakdown by system prompt, CLAUDE.md, tools, and conversation. In the exercise we will run it repeatedly to watch the numbers climb. Note: /context only reports — it is /compact that modifies the window. Docs for the memory behavior are at code.claude.com/docs/en/memory.

CLAUDE.md: A Context-Management Tool (Not a README)

You met CLAUDE.md in Session 2 as a place to describe your project. Today we reframe it:

💡

What CLAUDE.md really does

CLAUDE.md is loaded into the context window on every single turn, and it is never compacted away. Anything you put there is guaranteed to be present when Claude reasons about the next step.

Implication: put load-bearing rules there, not prose. Good CLAUDE.md content for a dissertation:

# Research protocol (load-bearing)

Research question: Does livestream commerce shift attention
away from product reviews? (PhD Ch. 2)

Inclusion criteria for citations:
 - Peer-reviewed, 2015 or later, in marketing or IS journals.
 - NEVER cite SSRN working papers in the main text.

Statistical conventions:
 - Report robust SEs clustered at the seller level.
 - Treat p < 0.05 as suggestive, p < 0.01 as confirmatory.

Here we break from Session 2's framing. There, CLAUDE.md was "a file where you describe your project." That understates what it does.

The key fact: CLAUDE.md is re-read fresh at the top of every turn, never edited, never summarized, never dropped. When compaction runs, CLAUDE.md is the only guarantee. That makes it qualitatively different from a README, a chat message, or any other way you could give Claude information.

Consequence: CLAUDE.md is the only place to put rules that must still be true at turn 200. Written in a chat message, "never cite SSRN drafts" becomes "be careful with citations" by turn 50. Written in CLAUDE.md, it is still there, verbatim.

So: CLAUDE.md is protocol, not documentation. Rules, conventions, inclusion criteria, validation thresholds — the kind of specific content the example shows. Not prose about your project history. That belongs in a README.

Compaction: What Claude Code Does When the Window Fills

When the window approaches capacity, Claude Code runs auto-compaction:

  1. Drop older tool outputs. Long file contents, Bash stdout, and grep results are cleared first.
  2. Summarize the conversation. Older user/assistant turns are replaced with a compressed summary.
  3. Preserve CLAUDE.md and the most recent turns verbatim.
⚠️

The research-integrity problem

Compaction is lossy. A methodological caveat you stated at turn 4 ("exclude papers with sample size below 100") may be silently summarized into "the user cares about sample size." If you rely on compacted context for analysis, you are relying on Claude's summary of your rules, not on your rules.

Auto-compaction has three steps. Older tool outputs get cleared first — usually enough to buy back room without touching the conversation. If more is needed, older turns get summarized. CLAUDE.md and recent turns are preserved verbatim. That is the only guarantee.

The research-integrity problem is what I want you to hold onto. "Exclude papers with sample size below 100" can get summarized into "the user discussed sample size thresholds." Technically true, methodologically useless — the threshold is gone, and at turn 50 Claude may apply a different one without telling you. Defense: put load-bearing rules in CLAUDE.md.

One warning: the auto-compaction threshold keeps moving as Anthropic tunes the harness. Do not trust a specific percentage. Rely on the technique — watch the status line, compact manually when you decide, not when Claude does.

Techniques 1 and 2: /clear and /compact

💬

/clear — nuclear reset

/clear

Empties the conversation entirely. CLAUDE.md stays; everything else goes. Use it between unrelated tasks (e.g., finishing a literature screen and starting a data-cleaning task).

💬

/compact — guided summary

/compact preserve the research question,
inclusion criteria, and list of papers
already screened; drop tool outputs
and verbose reasoning.

Summarizes the conversation with your instructions. Use it mid-task when you want to keep going.

🔑

Rule of thumb

/clear between topics. /compact within a topic. Never let auto-compaction decide for you if the work matters.

The two most important slash commands in Claude Code for research. Learn both. Use both.

/clear empties everything except CLAUDE.md. Use it between unrelated tasks — finishing a literature screen and starting data cleaning. Otherwise the new work inherits both the token cost and the "context contamination" of the old work.

/compact is the nuanced one. It accepts an instruction about what to preserve, and that instruction is where you encode what you care about. The example on the slide is realistic: keep the research question, inclusion criteria, and paper list; drop tool outputs and verbose reasoning. You iterate on the instruction over a project until you have a template you can paste in on demand.

Rule of thumb: /clear between topics, /compact within a topic, and never let auto-compaction happen silently on work that matters.

Technique 3: The Status Line as a Context Gauge

Claude Code displays a live bar at the bottom of your terminal. You do not write the script yourself — you ask Claude Code to build it for you, in natural language, and iterate until it looks the way you want.

💬

Ask Claude Code to build your status line

Create a custom status line for Claude Code. I want a progress bar that shows my context usage as a percentage, with the bar itself colored green below 50%, yellow between 50 and 70%, and red above 70%. Also show the model name at the left and the cost so far at the right.

Claude generates the shell script, wires it into ~/.claude/settings.json, and you iterate: "make the bar longer," "add emoji for the zones," "show the session name too."

💡

Three zones to watch

  • 0–50% (green) — Spacious. Work freely.
  • 50–70% (yellow) — Crowded. Start thinking about what to drop.
  • 70%+ (red) — Critical. Compact before the next big task.

The status line changes your habits more than any other technique. Until you can see the window filling, you cannot manage it. Once you can, you naturally start doing so.

The reframe on this slide: do not copy shell scripts from blog posts. Do not try to write a jq expression yourself. Just ask Claude Code in plain English for what you want. It knows its own settings schema, writes the script, and wires it into ~/.claude/settings.json for you. If the first version is not right, iterate: "make the bar longer," "add emoji," "print the git branch." One line each.

The visual design I recommend is a colored progress bar — green, yellow, red. The colors translate a number into a glance-able signal, so you do not have to think about whether 47% is fine. It is green, you keep going. Yellow, you finish the current subtask and /compact. Red, stop and deal with it before anything new.

Technique 4: Subagents as Context Isolation

A subagent is a Claude instance that runs inside a fresh, separate context window. Your main session dispatches it, it does its work, and it returns only its final message.

💡

What crosses the boundary

Parent → Child: the task you wrote ("Find every mention of construct X in these 40 files").

Child → Parent: a single summary message. Forty file contents, grep outputs, and reasoning traces never touch the parent's context window.

When to dispatch a subagent:

  • Verbose exploratory work (searching dozens of files, running long scripts).
  • Independent sub-problems that do not need the main conversation's history.
  • Any task where you care about the answer but not about the intermediate reasoning.

Subagents are the least-known technique but they change how you structure long sessions more than any other. The mechanic is simple: your main session dispatches a subagent, which runs in its own fresh context window, and only its final message comes back.

Verbose exploratory tasks are a context disaster. Ask your main session to "search forty PDFs for mentions of construct X" and each PDF gets read into your window; usage explodes. Dispatch a subagent for the same task and you get back one clean paragraph while the main window stays untouched.

Subagents are also useful for sub-problems that should not inherit your main session's accumulated opinions — "evaluate this statistical approach on its own merits." Less about tokens, same isolation property. Docs are at code.claude.com/docs/en/sub-agents. The stretch step in today's exercise uses them.

Technique 5: Save Useful Content to Disk

Anything you save to a .md file is outside the context window. It cannot be compacted. It cannot be summarized. It stays exactly as you left it.

💬

Ask Claude Code to save

Save the literature synthesis we just built to notes/session-03-synthesis.md, with one section per construct and a one-line note on each source.

Why this matters for research:

  • Intermediate outputs — tables, summaries, codebooks, draft sections — survive a /clear, a /compact, or a completely new session next week.
  • You can re-load them with a single prompt: "read notes/session-03-synthesis.md and continue where we stopped."
  • Combined with git, every saved file is a reproducible checkpoint of your AI-assisted reasoning — you can diff, you can revert, you can cite.
💡

The rule

Before you /compact, before you /clear, and always before you quit: save anything you would not want to re-generate.

The least-flashy technique on the list, and the one you will use most. The insight: the context window is working memory, and working memory is a bad place to store long-term research artifacts. Treat Claude Code the way you treat your own brain — when you figure something out, write it down.

Why is this context management and not just note-taking? Because it changes how aggressively you can use /compact and /clear. Once the valuable output is on disk, you cannot lose it. You can /clear the whole session and next week ask Claude Code to read the file — you are right back where you stopped, in a clean window.

The discipline: save proactively, at every natural checkpoint. Not reactively when the status line turns red. Finish a synthesis, save it. Settle on a coding scheme, save it. Draft a section, save it. Combined with git, every saved file is a reproducible checkpoint you can diff, revert, and cite. And use Markdown, not JSON — Markdown is for researchers plus Claude; JSON is for machines.

Putting It Together: A Context-Hygiene Workflow for Research

🔑

The six-step research-hygiene workflow

  1. Start fresh per research task. Do not inherit context from unrelated work.
  2. Put load-bearing rules in CLAUDE.md. Research question, inclusion criteria, conventions, validation thresholds. Protocol, not prose.
  3. Watch the status line. Know where you are in the 0%–50%–70% bands.
  4. Save valuable outputs to disk as Markdown. Before you compact, before you clear, before you quit.
  5. Delegate verbose work to subagents. If a task would balloon your window with tool output, send it out.
  6. /compact before 80%, with a protocol-preserving instruction. Never let auto-compaction silently edit your rules.

This is the slide to photograph. The six steps map to the techniques we covered, in a logical order. Steps 1–3 set up the session: fresh context, load-bearing rules, visibility. Step 4 is the externalization discipline — anything worth keeping goes to disk. Steps 5 and 6 are mid-session interventions: subagents isolate verbose work, /compact reclaims room.

This is a discipline, not a script. A five-minute question about one paper needs none of this. A three-hour systematic review needs all of it. The point is that context management moves from a feature you use to a habit you have — and the habit is what protects the rigor of your research.

Key Takeaway

🎯

Session 3 principle

Context is a finite, lossy resource. Manage it deliberately, or your research suffers silently.

  • The context window is the entire working memory of your AI assistant.
  • Effective context < nominal context. Always.
  • Six techniques, three layers of defense: preserve (CLAUDE.md, save-to-disk), manage (status line, /clear, /compact), isolate (subagents).
  • Anything you save outside the window cannot be lost.
  • Rigor, not convenience, is the reason to master this.

A deliberately compact summary. By the end of today, you should be able to articulate, without notes: what a context window is, why effective differs from nominal, what CLAUDE.md really does, what /clear and /compact are for, why the status line matters, why you save outputs to disk, and what a subagent is.

The three-layer framing is the cleanest mental model I have. Preserve: CLAUDE.md and save-to-disk — keep information where it cannot be lost. Manage: status line, /clear, /compact — control what is in the window. Isolate: subagents — keep verbose work out entirely. Different problems call for different layers.

One exhortation: this is the most useful 30-minute lecture in the course for your day-to-day work. Everything later — lit reviews, data cleaning, writing — is built on top of context management. With it, those techniques stay honest. Without it, they degrade silently.

Paper Discussion: Hong, Troynikov, & Huber (2025)

Context Rot: How Increasing Input Tokens Impacts LLM Performance
Chroma Research Technical Report, July 2025.
https://research.trychroma.com/context-rot

💡

Key Findings

  • Central claim: performance degrades non-uniformly as input length grows, across 18 frontier LLMs (Claude 4, GPT-4.1, Gemini 2.5, Qwen3), with task difficulty held constant.
  • Needle–question similarity: retrieval accuracy drops when the answer is semantically distant from the question.
  • Distractor interference: irrelevant-but-related content hurts more than random noise.
  • Haystack structure: coherent long text is sometimes harder than shuffled sentences — the model attends differently.
  • Conversational memory: LongMemEval shows degradation in multi-turn recall as context grows.
  • Methodology: GPT-4.1 as LLM-judge, aligned >99% with human judgment on calibration data; full replication code released.

Students should have read the paper. Discussion leader: ten minutes to present, twenty for us to discuss. Four things to surface in the summary.

One: the methodology. The authors hold task difficulty constant while varying input length — the cleanest move in the paper, because it avoids the usual confound in needle-in-a-haystack benchmarks.

Two: the LLM-as-judge (GPT-4.1 at 99% alignment with humans). Probe that in discussion — is 99% enough for your research standards? What about the 1% of cases where the judge disagrees?

Three: the six experiments are different lenses on the same phenomenon, each a clean mini-study — needle–question similarity, distractors, haystack structure, conversational memory, repeated words, non-uniform degradation.

Four: the models tested are frontier models you actually use — Claude 4, GPT-4.1, Gemini 2.5 — so the results apply to you directly. And the caveat: Chroma sells a vector database, so "long context is leaky" is consistent with their business model. The methodology is still clean and the code is open. Name the interest, then judge the work on its merits.

Discussion Questions: Hong, Troynikov, & Huber (2025)

  1. Trust in long-context workflows: The authors show that effective context is far below nominal across all frontier LLMs. How does this change your trust in a "dump the entire literature into Claude" workflow for a systematic review?
  2. LLM-as-judge validity: The grading uses GPT-4.1 aligned >99% with human judgment on a calibration set. Is that sufficient for your own research standards, and what about the 1% of cases where the judge disagrees?
  3. Commercial interest: Chroma sells a vector database, and the paper's conclusion is consistent with their business model. How do you weigh rigorous methodology against the author's incentive structure?
  4. Technique–finding mapping: Of today's six techniques (CLAUDE.md, save-to-disk, /clear, /compact, status line, subagents), which most directly addresses the kind of degradation Chroma measures, and why?

Four questions, four different kinds of engagement.

Question 1 is about translating the finding into practice. Push for specificity, not "be more careful" — answers should name a concrete workflow change.

Question 2 is the methodological probe. 99% alignment with humans sounds impressive until you ask whether the 1% disagreement is precisely the hard cases. Use this to push students on how they read a "rigorous methodology" claim from an industry paper.

Question 3 is the commercial-interest probe. The conclusion matches Chroma's business model. That does not make the paper wrong, but read it with the incentive frame in mind. Note that Chroma open-sourced the code — that is what makes the paper trustworthy despite the interest.

Question 4 is the bridge back to today. The answer involves subagents, save-to-disk, and /compact — the three defensive techniques, operating at different layers: isolation, externalization, compression. CLAUDE.md protects rules but does not reduce length; the status line is observation, not defense.

If time is short, cut 2 and 3 and focus on 1 and 4.
