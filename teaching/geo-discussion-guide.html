Part 1 · The Moment

Search is quietly being rewritten.

Google still holds roughly 90% of traditional web search. On that headline number, nothing has changed. But if you look at the slice of queries people now resolve without a ten-blue-links page — the questions answered inside ChatGPT, the summaries that land above Google's organic results, the follow-ups inside Perplexity — a different picture emerges.

Users are still looking things up. They are just looking them up in systems that return a paragraph, not a list.

The zero-click problem

When Google shows an AI summary, users click through far less. In a Pew field study of real-world searches:

Link-clicks nearly halved when Google showed an AI summary (from 15% → 8%). The AI box itself absorbed only ~1% of clicks. Most notably, ~26% of searches ended the session entirely — a classic zero-click outcome. Source: Pew Research field telemetry, cited in Chen et al. (2025).
Think about this

If a quarter of users are satisfied by an AI summary and never click through, the entire SEO business model — rank high, earn clicks, monetize attention — fractures. Publishers lose traffic. Advertisers lose impressions. And brands lose their most direct channel to speak to customers in their own voice.

The question is no longer "how do I rank on Google?" It is "how do I get mentioned in the answer?"

Part 2 · The Core Question

Does the SEO playbook still work — or do we need a new one?

For twenty years, the rules of winning attention online were remarkably stable. Know your keywords. Earn backlinks. Optimize page speed. Get on page one of Google. The game was called Search Engine Optimization (SEO), and it was played against a ranking algorithm that produced ten blue links.

AI search does not return ten blue links. It returns a paragraph with three to five citations. The entire topology of "visibility" has changed — from a ranked list a user can browse, to a synthesized shortlist the user never sees the sources of unless they click into a footnote.

THE OLD GAME — SEO

Ten blue links

  • → Optimize for keywords
  • → Earn backlinks for rank
  • → Target click-through rate
  • → Own the page-one listing
  • → Measure success in impressions
  • → Content strategy = long-tail coverage
THE NEW GAME — GEO

A synthesized shortlist

  • → Engineer for machine scannability
  • → Earn citations in authoritative sources
  • → Target inclusion in the answer
  • → Own the justification attributes
  • → Measure success in AI mentions
  • → Content strategy = shortlist dominance
Empirical Question

Before the paper, almost nobody had actually measured whether traditional SEO signals — domain authority, page rank, keyword optimization — still produce visibility when the query goes through an LLM's web-search pipeline. Chen et al. ask the simplest possible version of this question:

Do AI search engines cite the same sources as Google — and if not, where do they diverge?

Part 3 · Methodology

The audit pipeline

The authors built a reproducible comparison pipeline and ran it across many conditions. Every experiment below is a variation on this core loop: generate prompts → run them on engines → extract citations → classify and aggregate.

Five-stage audit pipeline
① Prompt generation. Ranking-style prompts ("Top 10 X brands in [region]") are generated and programmatically rewritten for experimental conditions — translated into 5 target languages, rephrased under 7 paraphrase templates, conditioned on 3 personas. All transformations use GPT-4o as an instruction-following rewriter (no web browsing).

The four AI engines + Google

Each prompt is dispatched in parallel to one or more web-enabled AI engines. Web-enabled matters: these are not pure LLM knowledge lookups — the models query the live web and cite what they find.

The Brand / Earned / Social taxonomy

Every cited URL is normalized to its registrable domain (https://www.bankrate.com/... → bankrate.com) and then classified by GPT-4o-search-preview into one of three categories. This three-way split is the central analytical frame of the entire paper — which means the story the data tells is shaped by this choice.

Brand

Official brand or retailer sites that directly provide products or services.

apple.com · chase.com · toyota.com · bestbuy.com

Earned

Independent editorial, review, or comparison outlets — third-party authority.

techradar.com · nerdwallet.com · forbes.com · consumerreports.org

Social

Community, forum, and user-generated content platforms.

reddit.com · youtube.com · quora.com · tiktok.com

A caveat worth holding onto

The Brand/Earned/Social schema is a constructed frame. A different team with finer-grained categories (news vs. review vs. e-commerce vs. corporate blog) would produce different absolute shares. The relative patterns across engines are more robust than the exact percentages.

Finding 1 · The Central Result

AI search is overwhelmingly Earned.

When the same ranking-style query ("Top 10 smartphones in 2024") is issued to Google and to GPT, the distribution of cited domain types looks completely different. Google returns a balanced mix of brand, earned, and social sources. GPT — and Claude, similarly — strips out social almost entirely and concentrates on editorial third-party outlets.

The effect holds across regions (US + Canada), across verticals (Consumer Electronics, Automotive, Software), and becomes the dominant signature of the paper.

The pattern, in one sentence

Across every tested combination of vertical × region, AI search is earned-heavy (often 70–90%) and social-sparse (often near zero), while Google remains balanced — with substantial brand and social presence alongside earned. The asymmetry is largest in consumer electronics, where GPT reaches 92.1% earned in the US versus Google's 52.4%.

Discussion

Why might AI engines prefer editorial over brand-owned content? Candidate mechanisms to weigh: (a) RLHF training rewards "balanced" or "authoritative" sources; (b) retrieval ranking quietly inherits Google's E-E-A-T signals; (c) LLMs treat synthesis as a journalistic task and default to journalistic sources; (d) brand pages tend to be promotional prose that retrieval systems down-weight. Which do you find most plausible?

Finding 2 · A Structural Shift

Social content nearly disappears.

This is the finding with the largest second-order consequences. On Google, Reddit, Quora, YouTube and similar community-driven platforms can be 15–25% of top-10 results for product-adjacent queries — a reflection of the fact that people often trust peer discussion more than a branded landing page.

When ChatGPT or Claude answer the same query, that share collapses to effectively zero. Perplexity is a partial exception — it does surface YouTube — but Reddit is absent almost everywhere.

Overlap of cited links, Google vs. GPT

Referenced link overlap at k=5 and k=10 between Google and GPT. Even where source-type distributions rhyme, the specific domains cited diverge — Jaccard often stays under 0.25 for product queries.

Domain classification shares across engines

The purple social slices are almost invisible for Claude and GPT. Perplexity retains a small social presence, driven largely by YouTube. Gemini sits between.
A consequential asymmetry

This is not just a stylistic difference. Community discussion is where lived experience, minority opinions, edge cases, and counter-consensus live. If AI systems are systematically stripping that layer out of discovery, they may be producing answers that feel more authoritative and less idiosyncratic — but also flatter, more homogenized, and more susceptible to the biases of a small set of editorial gatekeepers.

Finding 3 · Engine-Specific Ecosystems

Each AI engine lives in a different information world.

Suppose Claude, ChatGPT, and Perplexity all answer the question "Best family SUVs in 2024." How much do their cited domains overlap? The Jaccard similarity — the fraction of unique domains common to both systems — turns out to be strikingly low, often under 0.25.

In practical terms: a brand that dominates the citation pool on one engine can be entirely absent on another. Winning visibility on Perplexity says almost nothing about visibility on Claude.

Inter-engine domain overlap (Jaccard similarity)

Pairwise overlap matrix

Jaccard = |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B|. A value of 0.15 means only ~15% of the combined domain universe is shared between the two engines.

Domain-diversity breakdown (paper)

Each engine contributes a large fraction of exclusive domains. Only a narrow "core" (Car and Driver, Edmunds, Consumer Reports) appears across all three.

The same pattern appears in local services (auto repair, dentists, moving companies), where the fragmentation is even more pronounced — only a handful of review aggregators (homestars.com, opencare.com) appear in all four systems.

Think about this

If the information you see depends fundamentally on which engine you picked, consumer decisions start to carry a new kind of idiosyncratic risk. A shopper consulting Claude and a shopper consulting Perplexity may not be comparing the same brands — they may be comparing different universes of brands. What does "informed consumer" mean in that world?

Finding 4 · Fragility of Results

Translating the query changes the answer more than rewording it.

The authors run two sensitivity experiments. First: translate the same query into Chinese, Japanese, German, French, or Spanish — does the engine cite the same sites? Second: rephrase the query within English (add a justification constraint, compress to keywords, demand ranked order) — same question.

The result is clear. Paraphrases perturb results only modestly; language swaps restructure them wholesale — but how much depends dramatically on which engine you're asking.

Cross-language domain stability, by engine
Claude — The most stable engine across languages. Darker cells mean higher overlap between English and the target-language query's cited domains. Claude tends to re-use English-language authority domains even when the query is in Chinese or Japanese.

Four distinct behaviors

Claude

English anchor. Reuses a stable set of English-language authority domains across all tested languages. Think Forbes, TechRadar, Consumer Reports surfacing whether you ask in French or in Mandarin.

GPT

Full swap. Near-zero overlap across languages. Effectively taps a different website ecosystem per language — German queries hit chip.de, Spanish queries hit Spanish review sites.

Perplexity

Comparable to Google. Modest overlap with isolated vertical pockets. Brand lists are more stable than domain lists — the named products rhyme across languages even when the sources don't.

Gemini

Mixed, vertical-dependent. Noticeably higher overlap in some pairs (EN–DE laptops ≈ 0.32) but low elsewhere. Inherits Google's localization gradient given its search-grounding.

Paraphrase sensitivity — same language, different wording
Google — Moderately sensitive to paraphrasing, except when the rewrite is a pure format change (imperative/keyword list), which surprisingly yields the highest stability.
Takeaway for multilingual strategy

A brand that wants visibility in Japan cannot simply translate its English brand content and expect GPT to cite it — GPT will look for a Japanese earned source. But if the target engine is Claude, English-language authority may still be recognized. There is no single "multilingual SEO" solution; authority must be localized per engine.

Finding 5 · The Gravitational Pull

When you don't specify, AI gives you Coca-Cola.

The authors design a clean test for "big-brand bias." They take 50 unbranded prompts — "most popular cola brand," "best selling soda in the world," "top cola brands everyone knows" — and ask ChatGPT and Perplexity to answer. Every named brand is then mapped to one of three buckets: a curated list of 20 global majors (Coca-Cola, Pepsi, Dr Pepper, ...), a list of 20 niche/indie brands (Jones Soda, Faygo, Cheerwine, ...), or "other."

The result is a clean demonstration of the structural advantage large, well-covered brands enjoy in AI recommendations.

The irony

Perplexity — the engine with the most diverse source mix — shows the strongest big-brand bias in recommendations. Source diversity does not translate to recommendation diversity. The gravitational pull of prominence operates at the synthesis stage, not the retrieval stage.

Discussion

Is big-brand bias a bug or a feature? Three framings worth weighing:
(a) Information quality frame — Coca-Cola really is the most-mentioned cola; the model is accurately reflecting the world.
(b) Market-fairness frame — niche brands are being foreclosed from consumer consideration sets by a synthesis step they cannot influence.
(c) Welfare frame — consumers wanted "options they haven't heard of," and the model gave them options they already knew.

Finding 6 · Engine Identity Matters

The engine you pick matters more than the persona you are.

Across all experiments, one meta-finding emerges: variation between engines is consistently larger than variation within an engine across personas, paraphrases, or even freshness. In the banking experiment (three personas × four engines × ranking queries), who the engine is explains far more of the cited-domain distribution than who the user is.

Top-10 domains for bank queries

Bankrate and NerdWallet lead; CNBC, Forbes, Yahoo Finance follow. The financial-information ecosystem is anchored by a few evergreen editorial hubs.

Freshness varies by vertical

In consumer electronics, Claude's mean article age is ~117 days (median 62). Fresh enough. In automotive, the mean balloons to ~331 days — Claude and GPT lean on static "best-of" ranking pages that don't age well. Perplexity compensates with more YouTube and retail content, which is fresher but more commercially entangled.

Article age distribution by vertical × engine
Automotive is the freshness trouble zone. The reliance on editorial ranking articles means AI engines can confidently cite year-old comparisons as if they reflect the current market.

Four engine personalities

Claude Conservative authority

Most earned-heavy (~87% in brand queries). English-anchored across languages. Narrow domain set — consistent, authoritative, and slow to freshness-update.

ChatGPT Editorial canon

Most earned-heavy (93%+). Swaps site ecosystems completely across languages. Leans on Wikipedia + canonical review outlets (TechRadar, Tom's Guide, Wikipedia, AP).

Perplexity Diverse & commercial

Most heterogeneous — surfaces YouTube + retail (BestBuy, Cars.com) alongside editorial. Paradoxically shows the strongest big-brand bias despite source diversity.

Gemini Brand-tolerant hybrid

Most brand-friendly of the four — 25–30% brand share in verticals where others drop to 5–10%. Search-grounding inherits Google's localization gradient.

Part 5 · The Prescriptions

The GEO Agenda — six strategic imperatives

The descriptive findings anchor a prescriptive framework: six imperatives the authors argue brands must follow to remain visible in AI search. Click each to expand the reasoning.

Read these carefully. They are the industry-facing agenda the paper defends, and — because of the ktau.ai acknowledgment — they carry a particular framing that's worth noticing.

AI agents must parse, interpret, and cite information — they behave like clients of an API, not readers of a marketing brochure. Treat your website as an API: rigorous Schema.org markup for every entity (price, spec, warranty, rating), structured comparison tables, explicit pros/cons, bolded value propositions that a synthesis step can lift as "justification attributes."

The single most-consistent finding. Shift PR budget from owned content and social engagement toward a systematic campaign of third-party features, reviews, and expert commentary. Backlinks from top-tier authoritative domains are not a Google ranking factor anymore — they are the direct input into AI's trust judgment.

Claude/ChatGPT reward placement in the canonical earned-media set (Forbes, Consumer Reports, TechRadar). Perplexity rewards YouTube creator relationships + accurate retail listings. Gemini tolerates — even rewards — deep on-domain content. Allocate differently per engine based on where your category's audience is.

The language experiment settles this: you cannot win GPT in German by translating your English brand page. You need earned coverage in German-language publications. For Claude, English authority may still transfer. Multilingual GEO is dual-path — local-language earned media and English authority.

Ten blue links reward quantity. Shortlists reward extractable justification. Every product page should answer "why this one?" in a form a synthesizer can quote: "longest battery life," "most rugged build," "best value under $500." Structured comparison tables beat flowing prose.

For smaller brands, the head-of-distribution bias is a structural disadvantage. Strategy: over-invest in specialty earned media (niche publications the synthesizer has learned to trust for a specific category) and YouTube expertise, where authenticity substitutes for scale. The goal is to be Perplexity-visible first, with eventual migration to Claude/GPT.

Part 6 · A Critical Lens

What the paper gets right — and what to question.

Good seminar discussion of an industry-adjacent paper means reading it with both appreciation and skepticism. The empirics here are genuine and large-scale. The normative framing, though, is worth interrogating.

Strengths

  • ✓ Scale. ~1,000 prompts × 10 verticals × 5 languages × 4 engines is genuinely large.
  • ✓ Controlled factorials. Language, paraphrase, persona dimensions are varied systematically.
  • ✓ Dual metric. Jaccard on both domain sets and brand sets disentangles "what sources?" from "what recommendations?"
  • ✓ Reproducibility-minded. Exact engine versions, prompt templates, and classification procedures documented.
  • ✓ Honest limitations. Temporal specificity, classifier subjectivity, and black-box opacity are all flagged.

Open concerns

  • ✗ Industry framing. Acknowledged vendor partner (ktau.ai) sells exactly what the paper prescribes. Normative language ("dominate," "arms race") trends promotional.
  • ✗ No causal claims. Correlational audit — we learn what AI cites, not why. Mechanistic claims would require interventions.
  • ✗ Taxonomy load-bearing. Three-way Brand/Earned/Social split is doing a lot of analytical work. Alternative partitions (news vs. review vs. UGC) might tell a different story.
  • ✗ No consumer welfare analysis. Paper treats visibility as the outcome; the downstream question — do AI-biased answers produce better or worse consumer choices? — is untouched.
  • ✗ Ranking-style prompts only. Real users rarely ask "Top 10 smartphones" — they ask "phone with a good camera for my mom." Generalization is assumed, not demonstrated.
Reading posture

The descriptive half of the paper is solid; the prescriptive half is a marketing brief. Separate them in your mind. The finding that "AI is earned-heavy" is robust. The implication that "therefore brands should hire a GEO firm" is an inference with commercial weight behind it.
