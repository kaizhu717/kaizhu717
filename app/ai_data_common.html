Selective Participation in the AI Data Commons

AI systems rely on web content, but access is increasingly governed by voluntary crawler restrictions. The updated paper shows that publisher access is sharply selective by quality and by use: credible publishers restrict training more than search, while misinformation sources and political-pole outlets become relatively more accessible.

Evidence Map

The updated paper is organized around selective participation, not a single binary opt-out story. Quality, use, and timing jointly determine what remains accessible.

Quality selection

High-factual and high-credibility publishers restrict AI crawlers at much higher rates than low-factual sources. The gradient holds across MBFC ratings, content-quality indices, and domain prominence.

Use-specific access

Search-only restriction is nearly absent. The meaningful margin is training-only restriction: publishers preserve search-like access while blocking training-oriented crawlers.

Composition

When high-quality publishers restrict training, the training-accessible pool shifts toward low-factual, questionable, and political-pole outlets.

Timing

The training factual-rating gap opened first after the GPTBot announcement and later narrowed as search restrictions caught up by February 2026.

Bot taxonomy

The paper separates training-oriented crawlers, search/discovery crawlers, ambiguous bots, and legacy bots, then tests both clean and broad classifications.

Governance implication

The observed behavior points toward use-specific access design and compensated-training institutions rather than treating AI web access as a binary allow-or-block decision.

Selective access is the central object.

Publisher directives reveal who participates in the AI data commons and which AI uses they are willing to support.
